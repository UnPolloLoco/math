What is a One-Sided Limit?

Each side of a limit can be solved for individually. One-sided limits contain a superscript + or - on the c value to indicate which side it approaches from; c^{-} means c is approached from the left, negative side, and c^{+} means c is approached from the right, positive side.

\begin{aligned} \lim\limits_{x \to 0} f(x) &= \DNE \N \lim\limits_{x \to 0^{-}} f(x) &= -1 \n \lim\limits_{x \to 0^{+}} f(x) &= 1 \end{aligned}

Implications

Since a normal, two-sided limit can only exist if both sides of x=c approach the same value, the following is true:

\begin{aligned} \lim\limits_{x \to c^{-}} g(x) &\neq \lim\limits_{x \to c^{+}} g(x) \n \lim\limits_{x \to c} g(x) &= \DNE \end{aligned}

Of course, if the limit exists then that implies both one-sided limits are equal:

\begin{aligned} \lim\limits_{x \to c^{-}} h(x) = \lim\limits_{x \to c^{+}} h(x) &= L \n \lim\limits_{x \to c} h(x) &= L \end{aligned}

Summary

  • A one-sided limit is a limit which only approaches c from one side
  • One-sided limits are written as either \lim\limits_{x \to c^{-}} f(x) or \lim\limits_{x \to c^{+}} f(x)
  • c^{-} is approached from the left and c^{+} is approached from the right
  • \lim\limits_{x \to c} f(x) = \DNE if the corresponding one-sided limits aren't equal
  • If a limit exists, then it has to be equal to both of its corresponding one-sided limits
