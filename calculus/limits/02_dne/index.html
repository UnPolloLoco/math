What is DNE?

If a limit at a given x-value does not converge on one single y-value, then the limit "does not exist." That is abbreviated as \DNE, and is used as follows:

\lim\limits_{x \to c} f(x) = \DNE

It's important to understand that the arrow in a limit operator (the \to in \lim\limits_{x \to c} f(x) = L) means that c is being approached from both sides, not just from the left as it may initially seem.

Notice how the following table and graph do not converge upon a single y-value as x \to 0. Both branches head in opposite directions, so the limit does not exist; \lim\limits_{x \to 0} f(x) = \DNE.

x f(x)
-0.1 -2.5
-0.01 -25
-0.0001 -2500
0 \undef
0.0001 2500
0.01 25
0.1 2.5

One-sided limits, which are covered next, will provide a slightly more exact definition of what makes a limit \DNE or not.

Summary

  • \DNE means "does not exist"
  • If the left and right sides of x \to c approach different values, then the limit is \DNE
